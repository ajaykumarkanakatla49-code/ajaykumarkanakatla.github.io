PM Thinking in Action

How I Made Decisions

Deliverables are the output. Decision-making is the work. Here are the moments that defined how I ran this project.

🧠

My approach: integration over administration

Most project coordinators track tasks. I treated my role as making sure five specialist workstreams — design, calculations, costing, risk, and sustainability — told a consistent story at every review gate. When they didn't, I had to decide: is this a drafting error, a scope gap, or a fundamental design conflict? Getting that diagnosis right saved weeks. Getting it wrong meant rework.

Week 1
Structuring the team before any design work started
SituationSix engineers with different skill sets, no prior working relationship, and a brief with 11 parallel deliverable streams. No template for how to divide the work.
DecisionI mapped each deliverable to one owner rather than splitting tasks by concept phase — building expertise rather than starting fresh each time.
OutcomeHandover quality was high throughout. Each specialist built deep understanding of their discipline, making integration reviews significantly faster.
Week 5
When structural methodology conflicted with construction sequence
SituationThe structural design assumed a specific pour sequence for RC walls, but the construction methodology team had planned a different sequence to fit the 96-hour possession.
DecisionI held a joint session to surface the constraint first — the possession window was non-negotiable. The structural approach then adapted to match.
OutcomeAvoided a revision cycle costing 3–4 days. The integrated solution became the basis for Concepts 2 and 3.
Week 8
Deciding how much of Concept 1 to carry forward
SituationStarting Concept 2, the team wanted to reuse most of the Concept 1 framework. But the partition wall fundamentally changed load paths and hazard profiles.
DecisionI defined a "carry-forward checklist" — assumptions were inherited unless a specific design feature changed. Load cases and possession constraints were fixed; structural sizing and risks reviewed fresh.
OutcomeFaster concept initiation without compromising quality. The framework was adopted for Concept 3, making it the most efficiently started phase.
Week 19
Choosing between two costing methods for Concept 3
SituationThe costing lead flagged that SPTM gave a materially different figure from an alternative method for the U-Box configuration. Two valid approaches, different answers.
DecisionRather than pick one and move on, I documented both with the delta and reason for the difference — making it transparent for the optioneering comparison.
OutcomeThe optioneering study was stronger for it — the cost range was explicit, and stakeholders could see the methodology basis.
How I Prioritised Across 26 Weeks
1

Programme integrity over output perfection

A 90% complete drawing issued on time is more useful than a perfect drawing issued late. I kept a clear distinction between "good enough to integrate" and "good enough to issue."

→ In a possession-constrained project, a late output in one workstream blocks every other workstream downstream.
2

Blockers before new work

Every Monday I reviewed the action log before assigning new tasks. If something was blocked, that came first. New tasks could always wait a day. Unresolved blockers compound.

→ Two unresolved blockers by Week 3 taught me this quickly. They don't sit still — they grow.
3

Integration reviews before individual polish

I scheduled a cross-team integration check every fortnight. No one polished their own output until we'd confirmed it was consistent with everyone else's.

→ Caught three inconsistencies in Concept 1 alone before they became rework at the issue stage.
4

Risk and sustainability woven in — not added at the end

I treated DRA and sustainability as live documents updated with every design change, not retrospective checkboxes. When a design changed mid-concept, the risk log changed the same week.

→ CDM requires this as a professional obligation. Building the habit early separates a real project from a student exercise.

What I Navigated

Challenges & How I Solved Them

Real projects don't go to plan. Here's what went wrong and what I did about it.

⚡
Managing a live station environment with zero disruption tolerance
The Problem
Every design decision had a downstream implication for construction. We couldn't design in isolation — every choice had to be constructable within 96 hours with limited access at an operational station.
How I Handled It
I introduced a "possession feasibility check" into every design review. Before any concept element was finalised, I asked the methodology lead to confirm it was sequenceable within the window.
💡 No design had to be revised purely for constructability after final issue.
🔀
Keeping five independent workstreams consistent with each other
The Problem
By mid-Concept 1 I noticed the drawings assumed one slab thickness, the calculations used a different value, and the BoQ was costed on a third. All three were "correct" in isolation.
How I Handled It
I created a single "design parameter log" — a shared reference document that locked in agreed dimensions, loads, and assumptions. Any change had to go through me before adoption.
💡 Zero parameter inconsistencies found in Concept 2 or 3 integration reviews.
📊
Maintaining momentum across three sequential concept cycles
The Problem
After completing Concept 1 there was a natural dip in energy — the team felt the "main work" was done. Starting Concept 2 from scratch risked losing the momentum and discipline we'd built.
How I Handled It
I ran a short retrospective at the end of each concept — what worked, what slowed us down, what to carry forward. This gave the team a sense of completion and reset rather than just rolling into the next phase.
💡 Concept 3 was initiated faster and completed on the tightest schedule of the three.
What I Learned

Reflection & Growth

Honest assessment of what this project taught me — and what I'd do differently.

✅ What Worked Well

  • Owning integration as a PM responsibility — not assuming specialists would self-coordinate
  • Making the possession window a fixed constraint from day one, not a delivery detail
  • Keeping the action log live between meetings, not just updating it during them
  • End-of-concept retrospectives — small habit, significant impact on team momentum
  • Documenting costing ambiguity transparently rather than forcing a single answer

🔄 What I'd Do Differently

  • Establish the design parameter log at kickoff, not after the first inconsistency appeared
  • Build the carry-forward checklist before Concept 2, not as a response to the transition
  • Schedule stakeholder reviews mid-concept, not just at issue — earlier feedback loops
  • Quantify sustainability comparisons numerically from the start, not just narratively
  • Dedicate explicit time at the start of each week to dependency mapping, not just task review
